题目

Suppose the probability of a coin turning up heads is 0<p<1, and that we flip it 7 times and get {H,H,T,H,T,T,H}. We know the probability (likelihood) of obtaining this sequence is L§=pp(1−p)p(1−p)(1−p)p=p4(1−p)3. What value of p maximizes L§? What is an intuitive interpretation of this value of p?
Hint: Consider taking the derivative of logL§. You can also directly take the derivative of L§, but it is cleaner and more natural to differentiate logL§. You can verify for yourself that the value of p which maximizes logL§ must also maximize L§ (you are not required to prove this in your solution).

solution

当p=47p=\frac{4}{7}p=74​时logL(p)logL(p)logL(p)最大，求解：

logL(p)=logp4(1−p)3=4logp+3log(1−p)logL(p) = log p^4(1-p)^3 = 4logp+3log(1-p) logL(p)=logp4(1−p)3=4logp+3log(1−p)

dL(p)dp=4−7pp(1−p)=0,p∈[0,1]\frac{dL(p)}{dp} = \frac{4-7p}{p(1-p)} = 0 , p \in [0,1] dpdL(p)​=p(1−p)4−7p​=0,p∈[0,1]

f(w)=∑i=1n∑j=1n(a⊤iw−b⊤jw)2+λ∥w∥22,
where the vector is w=(w1,…,wd)⊤ and ∥w∥2=∑dk=1w2k−−−−−−−√ is known as the L2 norm. Compute the gradient ∇f(w).
Recall: the gradient is a d-dimensional vector of the partial derivatives with respect to each wi:
∇f(w)=(∂f(w)∂w1,…∂f(w)∂wd)⊤.
If you’re not comfortable with vector calculus, first warm up by working out this problem using scalars in place of vectors and derivatives in place of gradients. Not everything for scalars goes through for vectors, but the two should at least be consistent with each other (when d=1). Do not write out summation over dimensions, because that gets tedious.
